Catch · Typography study · v2 · legibility-first
Legible first. Editorial always.

Correcting the last pass: the Didones (Playfair, Bodoni) were the wrong family — beautiful as logos, but they sacrifice readability at text sizes. Since you want serif body text, the face has to read well at 15px and command attention at 50px. That points to screen-reading editorial serifs with optical sizing — shown here doing the hardest job first: a paragraph.

1 · Why the Didones failed — contrast vs legibility

Identical paragraph, 15px. High contrast = thin strokes thin out and shimmer ("dazzle"); moderate contrast holds. This is the readability tradeoff, made visible.

High contrast · hard to read
Long table, short questions
Curated tables of six. One long conversation, phones away. The thin strokes get fragile and the eye works harder line after line — fine for a masthead, punishing for a bio.
Moderate contrast · easy to read
Long table, short questions
Curated tables of six. One long conversation, phones away. Even strokes and a generous x-height keep the line steady and calm to read, paragraph after paragraph.

2 · The legibility levers (what actually drives the choice)

01 · Optical sizing
Sturdy small, fine large
A real text face has a "text" cut (sturdier, lower contrast, looser) and a "display" cut (finer, tighter). Using one face across both is the secret to refinement. All three candidates carry an optical axis.
Aa opsz 6 (text)   Aa opsz 72 (display)
02 · x-height
Taller lowercase reads smaller
A generous x-height keeps text legible at small sizes — critical for bios and meta on a phone. Reading serifs maximize it; display serifs often don't.
handgloves
03 · Moderate stroke contrast
Even color on the page
Low-to-moderate modulation gives an even "texture" across a paragraph and survives small sizes and dark mode. High contrast is reserved strictly for very large display.
Sundowner 5K
04 · Open apertures + weight
Letters stay distinct
Open counters/apertures (the a, e, c, s) prevent letters from clogging at size; a true text weight (~400–500) keeps body even, with heavier weights saved for display.
